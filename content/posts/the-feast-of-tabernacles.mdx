10 years ago we started a tradition... We started setting up our tent inside the house and sleeping in it on the night before [General Conference](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/general-conference?lang=eng). I had read about this idea on a blog way back then and I thought that it sounded fun and meaningful. It connected with a story from The Book of Mormon where the people set up their tents near the temple so they could hear King Benjamin (who was also the prophet at the time) speak to them...

> "And it came to pass that when they came up to the temple, they pitched their tents round about, every man according to his family, consisting of his wife, and his sons, and his daughters, and their sons, and their daughters, from the eldest down to the youngest, every family being separate one from another.
>
> And they pitched their tents round about the temple, every man having his tent with the door thereof towards the temple, that thereby they might remain in their tents and hear the words which king Benjamin should speak unto them;" ([Mosiah 2:5-6](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/bofm/mosiah/2?lang=eng))

I loved the symbolism of each household facing towards the temple and coming together to hear the Prophet speak so this little tent tradition stood out to me and we started it in our family when the next General Conference rolled around that October. The kids instantly fell in love with this new tradition and it's been something they look forward to every April and October since we have General Conference twice a year.

Fast forward to this year... about a week ago. It was 5 days before General Conference and in looking up something else, I stumbled across some really interesting connections about The Feast of Tabernacles. Even though I had heard about this Jewish holiday a little bit here and there before and the little bits I did know had made me want to learn more about it, I hadn't ever learned much about it in detail. This time though, my curiosity was piqued and I started diving deep into learning about it. I remember there being a part about it in an [episode of The Chosen](https://youtu.be/5jweRV5noEg?si=MPb5cvSIQcY9W1vO) so it was fun to go back and re-watch that. I also began to realize that this General Conference tent tradition that we had been doing for the past decade actually connected with The Feast of Tabernacles! That was really exciting for me to discover. Remember the story of King Benjamin's people? It's very likely that they had all come together to celebrate The Feast of Tabernacles. [Here is an article](https://scholarsarchive.byu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?filename=6\&article=1045\&context=mi\&type=additional) that talks about this. Let me back up a bit as well... the reason why I even stumbled across things about The Feast of Tabernacles was because I had been thinking about how it was recently the 200th anniversary of the [angel Moroni appearing to Joseph Smith](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/the-testimony-of-the-prophet-joseph-smith/moronis-visit?lang=eng#figure1_p1) and telling him about the Gold Plates and the coming forth of The Book of Mormon. When I was looking up things about that, I stumbled across a talk that mentioned that that first year when Moroni visited Joseph Smith, it was during the holiday, Sukkot (which is The Feast of Tabernacles), and how that was really symbolic and significant. So with that in mind, as I dove deep and learned more about The Feast of Tabernacles over the next few days, I had the [restoration of the gospel of Jesus Christ](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/manual/gospel-topics/restoration-of-the-church?lang=eng#p1) in the forefront of my mind and I was looking for connections between the ancient holiday and what it means for us today. So I'm going to share some of what I learned. I'm sure I've just barely scratched the surface though. Here are a couple of videos that I thought were very interesting and helped me to get a good overview\...

The more I learned about the Feast of Tabernacles, the more I wondered if/how we still celebrated it now in modern times, without me really recognizing it. I realized that we celebrate in at least 2 ways: Thanksgiving and General Conference. I had a vague memory of a quote from a book that I read a couple years ago and when I found it again I better recognized the significance and thought it was so fascinating as it had been right in front of my eyes the whole time.

> "The ancient covenant even played into the first Thanksgiving. It is widely believed that the feast was based on the Hebrew holiday of the Feast of Tabernacles. Biblically, it was a multi-day holiday, a time of praying, feasting, and giving thanks. That ancient feast, known as _Sukkot_ in Hebrew, was, according to historian Barney Kasdan, "to be a time of bringing in the latter harvest. It is, in other words, the Jewish 'Thanksgiving.'" The concept of giving thanks or _Thanksgiving_ is mentioned dozens of times in the Bible. William Bradford, a great student of the Bible, presided over the first American Thanksgiving and followed this biblical pattern. Like with Sukkot, Bradford made Thanksgiving a multiday holiday to celebrate the fall harvest and pray, feast, and give thanks to God. The Hebrew tradition, as per the book of Deuteronomy, directs that "thou shalt rejoice in thy feast, thou and thy son, and thy daughter...and the stranger." Dutifully, the Pilgrims invited "the stranger," even the local native tribes, to join in the festival of thanksgiving. And they came. No wonder Kasdan suggests that these early American settlers, "who were great students of the Hebrew Scriptures, based the first American Thanksgiving on Sukkot." (Timothy Ballard, The Pilgrim's Hypothesis, pg. 74-76)

I also [read an article](https://latterdaysaintmag.com/is-there-a-precedent-for-general-conference-in-the-book-of-mormon/) that pointed to General Conference connecting to the Feast of Tabernacles and I thought it was so interesting as well. It brought certain aspects of The Book of Mormon to life for me in a whole new way.

As I dove deep and read more and more about The Feast of Tabernacles, I started putting together the pieces in my mind and heart and this is what I ended up with... **Holy Convocation** -Convocation refers to a large formal assembly of people, in this case, a holy gathering. -This is very apparent in our modern General Conference where we get to hear messages from God through the living Prophet and apostles and other church leaders. -Another connection that I felt went into this category is going to the temple to receive instruction from God. The place to gather for the holy convocations was the temple. And now, in this dispensation where we have the restored church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, we can go to the [temple](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/temples?lang=eng) to spend time in God's presence and receive heavenly guidance and instruction. -Every 7 years, it was considered a Sabbatical year and all men, women, children, and visitors would gather to hear the priests and elders read from the scriptures (Torah). I don't know if this is referring to the entire Torah which is the 5 books of Moses (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy) or if it is only referring to a portion of the Torah but I did a tiny bit of research and calculations and these are some interesting statistics...

-for the average reader, reading silently at 300 wpm, it would take 6 1/2 hours to read the Torah (5 books of Moses)

-average reader, reading out loud at 183 wpm, it would take about 10 1/2 hours to read the Torah (5 books of Moses)

-“The Book of the Law” could also be referring to the first 30 chapters of Deuteronomy

-Interestingly, General Conference is 10 hours (5 sessions, 2 hours each, spread out over 2 days). That is very close to the same amount of time that it would take to listen to the 5 books of Moses being read aloud.

**Sacrifice** -If I am understanding correctly, more animal sacrifices were commanded by God to be performed during The Feast of Tabernacles than during any of the other holy days during the rest of the year. These sacrifices were symbols of Jesus's sacrifice that would come in the future. -Unless you are heartless, it is really sad to watch an animal die. Especially when you are the cause of it's death, either because of innocent mistakes, or because they have been raised as a food source. Both of these scenarios are something that I have experienced several times on our farm over the past few years of raising a lot of animals. It never gets easier to watch. It is always very humbling. I can imagine that witnessing so much death during the holy sacrifices would be heavy and sad and give even greater desire for the Messiah to gain victory over death. -I find it really humbling when I really recognize that something has to die in order for us to live. Even if someone is vegetarian or vegan and doesn't eat meat, there is still _a lot_ of death that happens in this world, in order for us to have food to eat. Think about how many fruit and vegetable plants are sprayed with pesticides, killing an incredibly huge number of insects. And even if you grow your own food and don't eat anything that has had pesticides sprayed on it, many insects are still killed in the process of harvesting. Even just harvesting honey (or just checking your beehives to see if they are in good health), it is very difficult to avoid a few bees dying in the process. And even plants often die in order for us to eat. I think that it was designed to be this way. I believe that all of this has been designed to point us to Jesus Christ. Just as we couldn't live physically without the something dying, we can't live spiritually without Jesus Christ's sacrifice, death, and resurrection. -Jesus Christ fulfilled the law of Moses, bringing an end to animal sacrifices. He no longer commands us to sacrifice animals. Instead, he commands us to offer our hearts to God and putting the animal in us on the altar.

**Gather** -gather harvest for feasts -gather with friends, family, strangers for meals -gather together for ceremonies, festivities, and convocations -gathering of Israel **Light** -in a time before electricity, large, bright lights (so bright they lit up all the courtyards in Jerusalem) would have provided a stark and exciting contrast to their usual dark nights. -God's presence returning to the earth, the light of the restoration, the priesthood, temples) -remember the pillar of fire in the wilderness and God's presence in the temple -light spread to all nations **Living Water** -in a time of no running water, they relied even more heavily on water from heaven. Life and prosperity depended on it. -The living water is the Holy Ghost. Water is symbolic of and vital to physical life and prosperity. The companionship of the Holy Ghost (experiencing God's presence) is vital to spiritual life and prosperity -pray for rain / the Spirit
