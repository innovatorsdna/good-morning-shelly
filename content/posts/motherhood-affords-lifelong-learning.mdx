<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

Do not let the world define, denigrate, or limit your feelings of lifelong learning and the values of motherhood in the home."

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

-perhaps our children are meant to raise us, as we are raising them.

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

January 12, 2022

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

"This week's study is about the Fall of Adam and Eve. As I was reading through the section in the Come Follow Me manual, a couple of thoughts came to me...

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

This is what the manual says...

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

"At first, the story of the Fall of Adam and Eve might seem like a tragedy. Adam and Eve were cast out of the beautiful Garden of Eden. They were thrown into a world where pain, sorrow, and death are ever present (see [Genesis 3:16–19](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/gen/3.16-19?lang=eng#p16)). And they were separated from their Heavenly Father. But because of the truths restored through the Prophet Joseph Smith in the book of Moses, we know that the story of Adam and Eve is actually one of hope—and an essential part of God’s plan for His children.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

The Garden of Eden was beautiful. But Adam and Eve needed something more than beautiful surroundings. They needed—and we all need—an opportunity to grow. Leaving the Garden of Eden was the necessary first step toward returning to God and eventually becoming like Him. That meant facing opposition, making mistakes, learning to repent, and trusting the Savior, whose Atonement makes possible progression and “the joy of our redemption” ([Moses 5:11](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/pgp/moses/5.11?lang=eng#p11)). So when you read about the Fall of Adam and Eve, focus not on the seeming tragedy but on the possibilities—not on the paradise Adam and Eve lost but on the glory their choice allows us to receive."

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

When I read the sentence "The Garden of Eden was beautiful. But Adam and Eve needed something more than beautiful surroundings." The thought that came to my mind was this... they needed to have children. Posterity is what eternal life is all about. We focus so much on Adam and Eve needing to come into this world so they could gain experience and learn and grow\... but I think that those things are directly related (and caused by) having children. What if Adam and Eve would have left the garden but not had children? Sure, they would have learned some things. They still would have had to figure out how to take care of themselves and keep themselves alive. But beyond that, life wouldn't have much meaning and the only person they would have to learn to get along with (which is a big part of our learning here on earth) would be their spouse. But even that would be relatively easy because they weren't being pushed and pulled in different directions by other people in the world. All they would really have to fight about was which direction to travel, what food to eat for dinner, what shelter to live in, etc. So having children was essential, not only to Heavenly Father's plan of bringing all of His spirit children to earth to get bodies...but also to having a _full_ experience here on earth. It was essential to _continuing_ life... _eternal_ life. Having children was the goal. It was the reason for leaving the garden. In our world today (where having children is becoming and less and less desired and valued), we forget that having children/posterity/increase was the main goal all along. It is having children that accomplishes both goals of bringing spirits to earth _and&#x20;_&#x67;iving us the full life experience. The family and home is God's university.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Here's another part from the manual...

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

"Adam and Eve’s Fall brought physical and spiritual death into the world. It also brought adversity, sorrow, and sin. These all seem like reasons to regret the Fall. But the Fall was part of Heavenly Father’s plan to redeem and exalt His children through “the sacrifice of the Only Begotten of the Father” (Moses 5:7)"

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

When I read this, I thought, what about looking at this the other way? "Adam and Eve's Fall brought physical and spiritual _LIFE_ into the world." Why do we focus so much on the "negative" aspect of it by saying they brought physical and spiritual _death_ into the the world? Yes, death was part of the deal... it always is when there is birth. Where there is birth, there is death. Either birth of something new causes the death of something old (this would be death and rebirth), or something is born and grows old and then dies (this would just be death). But in a way, if someone or something (person, plant, animal, etc.) passes on it's seed and those seeds are "born" and grow, then did the person/plant/animal actually _die_ completely? Part of them still lives on in their posterity. I think this is part of _eternal_ life.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Adam and Eve's choice to leave Eden (and then to procreate) led to physical life (through babies) and spiritual life (through the opportunity to experience spiritual rebirth throughout our lives, and also through giving us the chance at attaining eternal life).

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Yes, where there is life there is death... but also where there is death, there is life.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Was Adam and Eve's choice to partake of the fruit a bad choice that led to pain, sorrow, misery, and death?

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Or was their choice good, and it led to life, birth and re-birth, experience, wonder, curiosity, relationships, redemption, and joy?

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

It depends on who you ask. People who are not members of the church may say the former. ... but even members of the church could do better to put more emphasis and focus on the latter.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Seek and ye shall find. You will find what you're looking for.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

How does this currently apply to my life?

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Children are a _blessing_. When God told Adam and Eve to multiply and replenish the earth, he didn't _just_ command them to have babies. He _blessed_ them.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

"And I, God, _blessed_ them, and said unto them: Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth, and subdue it, and have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over every living thing that moveth upon the earth." (Moses 2:28)

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

It's so easy to get caught up in the daily grind of life and to forget that children are _blessings_. It's not just our _responsibility&#x20;_&#x74;o bring children into this world, it is our _privilege_ to.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Children are _gifts_ from God.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

This scripture just popped into my mind...

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

This is life eternal, that they might know thee, the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent." (John 17:3)

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

_This_ is life eternal. Knowing God. The way that we really come to _know_ God is through having children...and eternal life is, always has been, and always will be, about posterity, increase, the continuation of _life_, through seed. We come to know God in this way because this is how we become _like_ God. We get to experience love for our own children. That love doesn't always make things easy (in fact, challenges often result _from_ love... because we actually _care_ what happens.), but it is the core of God. God is love. And we get to have a little taste of that, and in the process become like God, through having children.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

This reminds me of something I read recently in a book I'm reading. It was talking about living in the present.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

"The foolishness of all this living in the future! like working very hard at something dull all your life so you can retire on plenty of money at eighty." (Brenda Ueland, If You Want to Write, pg. 38)

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

As a human, and especially as a wife/mother, I get so caught up in living in the past or the future. And while it _is_ important to remember (which often has to do with the past) and take care of things that need to be changed through repentance (because of choices in the past); And it is important to think ahead, have vision, and prepare and plan for the future... it is a tragedy when we spend too much time/effort/thought/feeling/focus/attention on the past and future. We miss life here in the present. _This_ is life. _This_. Right here. Right now. This time that I have with my children here at home, in these exact ages and stages is fleeting.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Savor it. Soak up every last ounce of it. Enjoy it for the _blessing_ that it is.

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

Another thought I just had while reading a story to Roxanne about Jesus and children...

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

While yes, part of my role as a parent is to lead and guide my children, I would do well to watch and observe _them_ and allow them to lead and guide me. I can learn a lot from my children. In fact, I am working to _become_ as a little child...what better way to learn how to do that than to emulate my children (the good things at least. haha)"

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->

-God is raising me as I do my (flawed) best to raise my children. And He will do the same for the next generation. He will raise my adult children as they do their best to raise their own children. And the cycle continues.
