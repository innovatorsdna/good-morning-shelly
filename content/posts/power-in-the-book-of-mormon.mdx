January 16, 2020 "There is a power that will come into your life the moment you begin a serious study of it." I was just standing here in the kitchen, making lunch and this quote came into my mind. I feel the truth of this quote. I just feel this underlying peace and joy. Is it because life is perfect right now? No. In fact all of my kids are currently sick and because of that I haven’t left the house for 4 days. The last time I stepped foot outside was on Sunday afternoon. It’s Thursday now. That’s a long time. Jershon got sick last weekend and is finally recovering but all of the kids have been sick the past few days. So far, I have miraculously escaped it and stayed healthy. They have fevers, and coughs mostly but they don’t feel good. Perhaps it’s a mild case of the flu? Anyway, back to the point... my life isn’t perfect at the moment (although it could certainly be much worse) but I _still_ feel this underlying/overarching feeling of peace and joy. And I am convinced that it is because I have been immersing myself in the Book of Mormon over these past couple weeks. ... really studying it. This feeling reminds me of how I felt when I was doing the Book of Mormon challenge in October -December 2018. I spent so much time in the Book of Mormon during those few months and I _felt_ the results of that during that time. And then I started the Book of Mormon again in January 2019 and set a goal to finish it by my birthday, which I did. That study wasn’t nearly as intense so although I still had a good experience with it, the good feelings weren’t _quite_ as potent. After my birthday, I got lazier and lazier. First I just read, not really doing much of a deep study, with no real goal of finishing it by a certain date. I only read a handful of verses every day and then even that slipped as I started using my study time to study some of the sections in the Doctrine and Covenants about the Priesthood (also a challenge given by President Nelson in October 2019). Even though I have enjoyed my Doctrine & Covenants study and gained a lot from it, I let my Book of Mormon reading/study slip during October-December 2019. And I didn’t really realize it at the time, but I realize it now\... I _felt_ the void of not being immersed in that book. It was a weird few months. I felt some level of depressed. I felt like I couldn’t get a handle on it. I thought it was just because I had started homeschooling and was dealing with the learning curve of all of that and figuring out how to deal with the mental stress (for an introvert especially) of having almost zero quiet time to myself every day. But now\...now that I am again immersed in the Book of Mormon and recognizing these familiar feelings (similar to October - December 2018), I realize that the "depression" (if that’s what it was) that I was feeling was caused by me not being immersed in the Book of Mormon. It’s a very interesting thing, this book. I don’t quite understand _how_ it holds the power that it does, but there is something about it that can’t be explained. Here is the complete quote from Ezra Taft Benson: "There is a power in the book which will begin to flow into your lives the moment you begin a serious study of the Book of Mormon. You will find greater power to resist temptation. You will find the power to avoid deception. You will find the power to stay on the strait and narrow path. The scriptures are called ‘the words of life’ (see D\&C 84:85), and nowhere is that more true than it is of the Book of Mormon. When you begin to hunger and thirst after those words, you will find life in greater and greater abundance" (Ensign, Nov. 1986, 7).

> "Thus it came that on a spring day in 1820 he knelt in prayer in the woods near his home to ask the crucial question - Which church is right? When he began to offer up his petition, the first thing that happened was that he was overwhelmed by a great power of darkness. He described this as not an imaginary force but the influence of a real being from the unseen world which had such marvelous power as he never before had felt in any being. So total was this power that he was about to abandon himself to it, when he, in a sense, turned a key of deliverance, for he called upon the name of the Lord. As soon as he had done so a light descended; and as soon as the light was there, he was released from the power of darkness. To me there has always been a lesson in that experience. While light and virtue and truth at times will not endure the presence of darkness and evil and will not, by choice, stay in their presence, ultimately the powers of darkness cannot endure the presence of light. By analogy, this can be demonstrated to a degree with electricity. We are able to run a wire into a room and through a system of connections and switches turn on a light. The instant that happens, the darkness is gone. Some little of it may hide as shadows under the furniture; but wherever the light can penetrate, the darkness must vanish. In other words, we have the capacity to introduce light into a room; and, as we do, darkness must dissipate. I do not know of anyone who can do the opposite- that is, introduce such darkness into a room that light will vanish. The ultimate power rests with the Lord and with His priesthood, with His servants. That idea should be an encouragement to members of the Church when they are intruded upon by forces that are evil and dark. Ultimately the power of light can hold them in abeyance." (Boyd K. Packer, The Holy Temple, pg. 122-123)
