I am an introvert. I have always been more reserved, and as a child, I was considered to be very "shy." I was so shy as a little girl that I had to go to summer school _before_ kindergarten, because I wouldn't talk to the teachers at my my kindergarten screening, so they had no idea what I knew and didn't know academically and they didn't know how to assess me. When I was a senior in high school, I was voted (for the yearbook) as "Most likely to be seen and not heard." At the time, I was just excited that I got voted for the yearbook at all. But since then, I have seen this kind of as more of an insult than a compliment.\
\
As I have gotten older, my social skills have improved and I am much more comfortable talking to people than I used to be, although I still prefer one-on-one or small groups and I still get drained sometimes when I interact with people for an extended period of time. I need alone time to recharge.\
\
As I have matured and learned more about myself, especially over the past few years, I have developed more understanding and gratitude for my quieter, more reserved self. There are times though, when I still wish that I were more outgoing, more social, more friendly, more fun. And while I think that it is possible for me to develop more of those characteristics, I also think that it's important for me to recognize the strength in my own natural gifts.\
\
I feel like it is important to recognize the strengths and gifts that come along with being an introvert, 1, because I am one, and 2, because I am raising 2 of them (possibly 3...we'll find out when R gets a little older.) My two sons, L and P, are introverts. They are only 7 and 5 years old, but I have already had many experiences with them that have tested my own ability to love being an introvert. I find myself battling this internal seesaw.\
\
On one side, I see the strengths and gifts that my sons have to offer as their introverted, quieter, more reserved selves. And I want to stand up for them and say to the world "It's ok that they are quiet! It's ok that they prefer to be home and have alone or one-on-one time. Introverts are just as valuable and needed as extroverts!"\
\
But on the other side, I really struggle with wanting them to be different, probably because there are still pieces of myself that feel flawed and like I am not enough, and I want to protect my kids from feeling like that too. I want people to like them. I want them to like people. I want them to have friends. The ability to talk to people is a very important skill - for pretty much every area of life. And I think that some of these feelings and desires are good and that growth in certain areas is necessary. Like I said, it is something that I have gotten a lot better at over time, but I know that I still have a lot of room for improvement as well. I think that I still struggle with figuring out how much of me is perfect, just as I am, and how much of me needs to change and be different. ...and therefore, I have the same kinds of thoughts and feelings about my introverted boys.\
\
And then there is the other piece of the mental equation - Are they this way _because_ of me? While I know that we are all born with natural gifts and personalities (nature), I also realize that nurture has a big impact as well. I think this is one of the hard things about parenthood - watching you children imitate you... especially when they imitate your flaws and weaknesses.\
\
For example, when someone (usually adults, but even kids sometimes) tries to strike up a conversation with one of my sons, and the conversation ends up being very one sided (the adult talking, and my son averting his eyes, not answering (or barely answering) and looking really uncomfortable,) I have several thoughts and emotions (mostly negative) run through me. I feel embarrassed (for myself and my son... feeling like this is a reflection of my bad parenting), I feel frustrated (feeling like my son is being disrespectful), I feel protective ("just stop talking to him already! Can't you see that he's uncomfortable?"), and sometimes I even feel mildly angry ("isn't it ok to be quiet and reserved and not interested in having a conversation with a stranger or minor acquaintance? Does everyone _have_ to talk to people all the time?")
